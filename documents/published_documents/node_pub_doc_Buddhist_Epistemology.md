## Key Concepts (extracted from DOC_BE_01)

| No. | Node code | Concept | Pali / Sanskrit | Category | Definition |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | N_BE_00001 | Valid cognition | Pramāṇa (Skt.) | Foundation | A reliable source of knowledge; the means by which correct cognition is attained. Structured with four integrated components: means of knowing (pramāṇa), valid cognition (pramā), object to be known (prameya), and resultant cognition (pramāṇaphala). |
| 2 | N_BE_00002 | Direct perception | Pratyakṣa (Skt.) | Source of knowledge | Immediate cognition through the senses, prior to inference or conceptual overlay. Dignāga defines it as "perception (pratyakṣam) is devoid of mental construction (kalpanāpodham)." It apprehends the particular (svalakṣaṇa) as pure sensation without conceptual structure. |
| 3 | N_BE_00003 | Inference | Anumāna (Skt.) | Source of knowledge | Indirect knowledge obtained through logical reasoning based on a sign (liṅga). Vasubandhu defines it as "the observation of an object not occurring without [the probandum] for someone who knows that." It operates on the universal (sāmānyalakṣaṇa). |
| 4 | N_BE_00004 | Testimony | Āgama / Śabda (Skt.) | Source of knowledge | Knowledge derived from a reliable authority; in Buddhism, the word of the Buddha. Dignāga rejects it as an independent means of knowledge, treating verbal cognition as a variety of inference (anumāna). |
| 5 | N_BE_00005 | Object of cognition | Prameya (Skt.) | Foundation | The reality that serves as the object of valid knowledge. Dignāga maintains exclusive duality: svalakṣaṇa as the object of perception and sāmānyalakṣaṇa as the object of inference. |
| 6 | N_BE_00006 | Erroneous cognition | Bhrānti (Skt.) | Epistemic error | A mistaken apprehension of reality; an inversion of the true nature of an object. In the conventional world, mistaking a mirage for water is mithyā-saṃvṛti (erroneous conventional truth). |
| 7 | N_BE_00007 | Doubt | Saṃśaya (Skt.) | Epistemic error | [uncertain] A state of indecision in which neither alternative is definitively established. The pursuit of certainty (nirṇaya) requires some initial doubt (saṃśaya) or desire to know (jijñāsā) as its motivation. (Note: không trực tiếp từ nguồn này.) |
| 8 | N_BE_00008 | Conceptual construction | Vikalpa / Kalpanā (Skt.) | Mental structure | Conceptualized, categorizing cognition that applies names and classes — contrasted with bare perception. The mind superimposes structures like proper names (yadṛcchā-śabda), genus-words (jāti-śabda), quality-words (guṇa-śabda), action-words (kriyā-śabda), and substance-words (dravya-śabda). |
| 9 | N_BE_00009 | Non-conceptual perception | Nirvikalpaka pratyakṣa (Skt.) | Mental structure | Pure, direct perception free from conceptual or linguistic elaboration (nirvikalpaka, avyapadeśya). The object is the extensionless particular moment that disappears as soon as it appears. |
| 10 | N_BE_00010 | Mental perception | Mānasa pratyakṣa (Skt.) | Type of consciousness | Inner cognition through mental consciousness, independent of the five external sense faculties. In Yogācāra, one of the eight modes of consciousness (vijñāna). |
| 11 | N_BE_00011 | Self-awareness | Svasaṃvedana (Skt.) | Type of perception | [uncertain] The reflexive dimension of consciousness by which the mind is aware of its own states. Dignāga expounds this theory on the basis that the object of cognition (ālambana) is nothing other than the appearance of an object in cognition itself. (Note: không trực tiếp từ nguồn này.) |
| 12 | N_BE_00012 | Yogic perception | Yogipratyakṣa (Skt.) | Type of perception | [uncertain] Direct, supra-ordinary perception arising from deep meditative cultivation; seeing ultimate reality. A mode of transcendental (alaukika) perception not involving external objects and sensory faculties. (Note: không trực tiếp từ nguồn này.) |
| 13 | N_BE_00013 | Particular / Unique mark | Svalakṣaṇa (Skt.) | Ontology–cognition | The individual, unique characteristic of a thing; the object of direct perception. Per Dharmakīrti: (a) has power to produce effects (arthakriyākāritva), (b) is specific (asādhāraṇa), (c) is not denotable by a word (śabdasyāviṣaya), (d) is apprehensible without depending upon other factors. |
| 14 | N_BE_00014 | Universal / General mark | Sāmānyalakṣaṇa (Skt.) | Ontology–cognition | A shared, general characteristic; the object of inference and conceptual thought. Per Dharmakīrti: (a) has no power to produce effects, (b) is common to many things, (c) is denotable by a word, (d) is not apprehensible without depending upon other factors such as verbal conventions. |
| 15 | N_BE_00015 | Exclusion | Apoha / Anyāpoha (Skt.) | Philosophy of language | Meaning is conveyed by excluding what something is not (e.g., not-non-cow → cow). Dignāga's theory: "a word expresses its own object through the exclusion of the other [things], just as an inferential mark establishes the object to be proved through exclusion." Verbal cognition is not an independent pramāṇa but a variety of inference. |
| 16 | N_BE_00016 | Sign / Logical reason | Liṅga / Hetu (Skt.) | Logic | The inferential mark or logical reason used to derive a conclusion (e.g., smoke → fire). A property (dharma) located in the property-possessor (dharmin, pakṣa) that serves as evidence for inferring the probandum (sādhyadharma, liṅgin). |
| 17 | N_BE_00017 | Thesis | Pakṣa (Skt.) | Logic | The proposition to be established in a Buddhist syllogism; also the argument's subject (dharmin), the locus where both the logical reason (hetu) and the property to be established (sādhya) co-occur. |
| 18 | N_BE_00018 | Triple-condition syllogism | Trairūpya (Skt.) | Logic | The three conditions for a valid reason (trirūpa-hetu): (i) pakṣadharmatva — the reason must be a property of the subject; (ii) anvaya / sapakṣe sattvam — presence in similar cases (positive concomitance); (iii) vyatireka / vipakṣe 'sattvam — absence in dissimilar cases (negative concomitance). |
| 19 | N_BE_00019 | Pervasion | Vyāpti (Skt.) | Logic | The logical relation of pervasion between probans (hetu) and probandum (sādhya) that guarantees successful inference. Dignāga introduced this concept but did not explain how the relation could be universalized — a gap later addressed by Dharmakīrti's svabhāvapratibandha. |
| 20 | N_BE_00020 | Necessary relation | Avinābhāva (Skt.) | Logic | Vasubandhu's principle: "not-being-without" — the relation of indispensability between the ground (hetu) and the property to be established (sādhya). Defined as "a property which does not occur without a such (= which is inseparably connected with a probandum)." |
| 21 | N_BE_00021 | Essential relation | Svabhāvapratibandha (Skt.) | Logic | Dharmakīrti's innovation providing universal foundation for inference. Two types: tadutpatti (causality — relation between effect and cause) and tādātmya (identity — relation between two essentially common individuals, e.g., vṛkṣa/tree and siṃśapā/oak). Replaces Dignāga's purely inductive vyāpti with a deductive, ontology-grounded relation. |
| 22 | N_BE_00022 | Causal efficacy | Arthakriyā (Skt.) | Ontology–cognition | Double meaning: (1) Ontological: causal efficacy as the criterion of reality — "That which is able to perform a function exists ultimately." Only objects able to participate causally in producing phenomena are real. (2) Epistemological: to fulfill a practical purpose — valid cognitions provide the cognitive basis for successful human activities (puruṣārthasiddhi). |
| 23 | N_BE_00023 | Ignorance | Avidyā (Skt.) / Avijjā (Pāli) | Epistemic obstacle | Fundamental non-knowing of the true nature of reality; the root of suffering (duḥkha) and cyclic existence (saṃsāra). It generates the dogmatic belief in a permanent self (satkāyadṛṣṭi) which causes self-clinging (ātma-sneha). |
| 24 | N_BE_00024 | Wisdom | Prajñā (Skt.) / Paññā (Pāli) | Liberation | Penetrating insight into the nature of reality; the path out of ignorance. In the Buddha's schema, it is the third of the three trainings: virtue (śīla), concentration (samādhi), and wisdom (prajñā). The Buddha is designated pramāṇabhūta — the possessor of ultimate valid knowledge. |
| 25 | N_BE_00025 | Emptiness | Śūnyatā (Skt.) | Ultimate object | The absence of inherent existence in self and phenomena; ultimate reality according to Madhyamaka. Nāgārjuna's metaphysical essencelessness (niḥsvabhāvatā, dharmanairātmyavāda) against realism of all varieties. |
| 26 | N_BE_00026 | Two truths | Dvisatya (Skt.) | Ontology–cognition | Conventional truth (saṃvṛtisat, prajñaptisat) — the conceptually constructed phenomenal world; and ultimate truth (paramārthasat, dravyasat) — the dynamically subtle, spatio-temporally unstructured, non-conceptual, irreducible reality. Rooted in the Sautrāntika view established in Vasubandhu's Abhidharmakośa-bhāṣya. |
| 27 | N_BE_00027 | Inductive reasoning | Svārthānumāna (Skt.) | Logic | "Inference for oneself" — a private inferential cognition based on observation of an evidential sign (liṅga) fulfilling the three conditions (trairūpya). Endowed with personal psychological characteristics; the process begins with perceptible observation (darśana) of the sign. |
| 28 | N_BE_00028 | Deductive reasoning | Parārthānumāna (Skt.) | Logic | "Inference for others" — syllogistic reasoning communicating new knowledge to another person. Its purpose is "to produce in the opponent the same kind of ascertainment that is obtained by the proponent through an inference (svārthānumāna)." Taken in a metaphorical sense (upacāra). |
| 29 | N_BE_00029 | Momentariness | Kṣaṇabhaṅgavāda (Skt.) | Ontology–cognition | The Sautrāntika doctrine: "a moment disappears as soon as it appears without duration." The radical culmination of the process philosophy, conceptually establishing the fluxional character of both mental and physical reality. Rooted in the Buddha's dynamic law of dependent arising (pratītyasamutpāda). |
| 30 | N_BE_00030 | Means of valid cognition | Pramāṇabhūta (Skt.) | Foundation | The epithet of the Buddha: the possessor of valid method, arguments, meaning, practice, and purpose. Dignāga's guiding principle: one should accept a statement only after testing its validity, not out of reverence — "just as a goldsmith accepts the purity of gold only by testing it in fire, cutting it, and carefully testing it on a touchstone." |
