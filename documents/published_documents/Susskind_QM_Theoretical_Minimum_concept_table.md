> 8 concepts được gắn cờ `[NGOẠI SUY]` — nội dung suy từ kiến thức QM chuẩn, không trích từ văn bản gốc.

| No. | Concept | Category | Definition |
|-----|---------|----------|------------|
| 1 | Qubit | Quantum Foundations | Quantum analogue of a classical bit. A two-state quantum system — the simplest possible quantum system. Represented by a normalized vector in a 2-dimensional complex Hilbert space. Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit can exist in superpositions of its basis states. |
| 2 | Quantum State | Quantum Foundations | A complete description of a quantum system — everything that can in principle be known about how the system was prepared. Represented mathematically by a ket-vector \|Ψ⟩ in a Hilbert space. Knowing a quantum state does not guarantee predictability of all measurement outcomes. |
| 3 | Classical vs Quantum State | Quantum Foundations | Classical states are elements of a set; quantum states are vectors in a Hilbert space. Classical logic is Boolean (AND/OR/NOT on subsets); quantum logic involves non-commuting propositions. States and measurements are the same thing classically; they are distinct in quantum mechanics. |
| 4 | Hilbert Space | States & Vectors | The vector space of quantum states. A complex vector space (finite or infinite dimensional) equipped with an inner product. The state of any quantum system is a ray in this space. Vectors are called kets \|A⟩; dual vectors are called bras ⟨B\|. |
| 5 | Ket Vector \|A⟩ | States & Vectors | A vector in Hilbert space representing a quantum state. Can be written as a column vector of complex components: (α₁, α₂, …, α_N)^T. Must be normalized: ⟨A\|A⟩ = 1. The sum of any two kets is also a ket (superposition principle). |
| 6 | Bra Vector ⟨B\| | States & Vectors | The dual (Hermitian conjugate) of a ket vector. Represented as a row vector whose elements are the complex conjugates of the corresponding ket components: ⟨B\| = (β₁^*, β₂^*, …, β_N^*). The inner product ⟨B\|A⟩ yields a complex number. |
| 7 | Inner Product ⟨B\|A⟩ | States & Vectors | The complex scalar product between bra ⟨B\| and ket \|A⟩. Its squared magnitude \|⟨B\|A⟩\|² gives the probability of finding system in state \|B⟩ when prepared in \|A⟩. Linear in the ket and anti-linear in the bra. |
| 8 | Orthogonality | States & Vectors | Two state vectors \|A⟩ and \|B⟩ are orthogonal if ⟨B\|A⟩ = 0. Orthogonal states are physically distinguishable by a single measurement. Orthogonality is the quantum analogue of disjoint subsets in classical state space. |
| 9 | Orthonormal Basis | States & Vectors | A set of N mutually orthogonal, normalized vectors \|i⟩ spanning an N-dimensional Hilbert space: ⟨j\|i⟩ = δ_ij. Any state can be expanded as \|A⟩ = Σ_i α_i\|i⟩ with components α_i = ⟨i\|A⟩. |
| 10 | Superposition | Quantum Foundations | Any linear combination of ket vectors is a valid ket vector. A qubit state is a superposition of basis states: \|Ψ⟩ = α\|0⟩ + β\|1⟩. This is the source of quantum interference and the fundamental difference from classical mixtures. |
| 11 | Phase Factor | States & Vectors | A complex number of the form e^{iθ}. Multiplying a state vector by a global phase factor e^{iθ} produces a physically identical state (phase ambiguity). No measurable quantity depends on the global phase. Relative phases between components are physically significant. |
| 12 | Normalization | States & Vectors | The condition that ⟨Ψ\|Ψ⟩ = 1, ensuring total probability sums to unity. Physically meaningful state vectors must be normalizable. For a spin state α_u\|u⟩ + α_d\|d⟩: \|α_u\|² + \|α_d\|² = 1. |
| 13 | Spin | Spin Systems | An intrinsic degree of freedom of a particle — the simplest and most purely quantum-mechanical system. Described by three component operators σ_x, σ_y, σ_z. Measurement along any axis yields only ±1 (in appropriate units). Serves as the prototypical qubit. |
| 14 | Pauli Matrices | Spin Systems | The three 2×2 Hermitian matrices representing spin component operators: σ_x = [[0,1],[1,0]], σ_y = [[0,-i],[i,0]], σ_z = [[1,0],[0,-1]]. Together with the identity I, they span the space of all 2×2 Hermitian matrices (any spin observable). |
| 15 | Spin Polarization Principle | Spin Systems | Any state of a single spin is an eigenvector of σ·n̂ for some direction n̂ with eigenvalue +1. Every spin state is characterized by a polarization vector. No state exists for which expectation values of all three components vanish. |
| 16 | Spin Component States \|u⟩, \|d⟩, \|r⟩, \|l⟩, \|i⟩, \|o⟩ | Spin Systems | The six basis states for spin measurements along the three axes: \|u⟩/\|d⟩ for z-axis (σ_z = +1/–1), \|r⟩/\|l⟩ for x-axis (σ_x = +1/–1), \|i⟩/\|o⟩ for y-axis (σ_y = +1/–1). Expressed in the σ_z basis: \|r⟩=(1/√2)(\|u⟩+\|d⟩), \|l⟩=(1/√2)(\|u⟩−\|d⟩), \|i⟩=(1/√2)(\|u⟩+i\|d⟩), \|o⟩=(1/√2)(\|u⟩−i\|d⟩). |
| 17 | Observable | Operators & Observables | Any measurable physical quantity. In quantum mechanics, every observable is represented by a Hermitian (self-adjoint) linear operator L acting on Hilbert space. Examples: spin components, position, momentum, energy (Hamiltonian). |
| 18 | Hermitian Operator | Operators & Observables | A linear operator M satisfying M† = M (equal to its own Hermitian conjugate). Hermitian operators have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors — necessary properties for representing observables whose measurement outcomes are real numbers. |
| 19 | Hermitian Conjugate M† | Operators & Observables | The combined operation of complex conjugation and transposition: M† = (M^T)^*. If M\|A⟩ = \|B⟩ then ⟨A\|M† = ⟨B\|. Represents the dual action of an operator on bra vectors. |
| 20 | Eigenvalue / Eigenvector | Operators & Observables | If M\|λ⟩ = λ\|λ⟩, then λ is an eigenvalue and \|λ⟩ is an eigenvector of M. The eigenvalues of an observable are the possible results of measuring that observable. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal. |
| 21 | Linear Operator | Operators & Observables | A mathematical machine that acts on a ket to produce another ket: M\|A⟩ = \|B⟩, satisfying linearity: M(z₁\|A⟩ + z₂\|B⟩) = z₁M\|A⟩ + z₂M\|B⟩. Represented by a matrix in a chosen basis. |
| 22 | Expectation Value ⟨L⟩ | Operators & Observables | The statistical average of measurement outcomes for observable L in state \|Ψ⟩: ⟨L⟩ = ⟨Ψ\|L\|Ψ⟩. Computed by summing eigenvalues weighted by their Born-rule probabilities: ⟨L⟩ = Σ_i λ_i P(λ_i). |
| 23 | Commutator [A, B] | Operators & Observables | [A, B] = AB – BA. Measures the failure of two operators to commute. If [A, B] = 0, A and B can be simultaneously diagonalized and measured with certainty. If [A, B] ≠ 0, they are subject to an uncertainty relation. |
| 24 | Commutation Relations for Spin | Spin Systems | [σ_x, σ_y] = 2iσ_z, [σ_y, σ_z] = 2iσ_x, [σ_z, σ_x] = 2iσ_y. No two components of spin commute — they cannot be measured simultaneously with certainty. |
| 25 | Measurement | Measurement | The physical act of determining the value of an observable using an apparatus A. A measurement yields one of the eigenvalues of the corresponding Hermitian operator. Unlike classical measurements, quantum measurements are generally not gentle — they disturb the system and change the state. |
| 26 | Born Rule (Principle 4) | Measurement | The probability of obtaining eigenvalue λ_i when measuring observable L on state \|A⟩ is P(λ_i) = \|⟨λ_i\|A⟩\|² = ⟨A\|λ_i⟩⟨λ_i\|A⟩. The squared magnitude is used because probabilities must be real, non-negative numbers, while inner products can be complex. |
| 27 | State Preparation | Measurement | The first interaction with measurement apparatus A prepares the system in one of the eigenstates of the measured observable. Subsequent identical measurements confirm that state. This is the quantum analogue of "seeing" a classical state. |
| 28 | Four Principles of QM | Quantum Foundations | 1. Every observable is identified with a Hermitian linear operator. 2. Possible measurement results are the eigenvalues of that operator. 3. Unambiguously distinguishable states are represented by orthogonal vectors. 4. The probability of outcome λ_i is \|⟨λ_i\|Ψ⟩\|². |
| 29 | Quantum Logic (vs Boolean) | Quantum Foundations | In quantum logic, propositions (A or B) and (B or A) can yield different truth values because measuring A disturbs B when operators don't commute. The distributive law of Boolean logic fails. State space is a vector space, not a set. |
| 30 | Time Evolution (Unitarity) | Dynamics & Time Evolution | Quantum states evolve via a unitary operator U(t): \|Ψ(t)⟩ = U(t)\|Ψ(0)⟩. Unitarity (U†U = I) ensures conservation of probability and distinguishes states. Information is never lost — two initially distinct states remain distinct. |
| 31 | Hamiltonian H | Dynamics & Time Evolution | The Hermitian operator representing the total energy of a system. Generates time evolution. The time derivative of any observable L is d⟨L⟩/dt = –(i/ħ)⟨[L,H]⟩. Determines dynamics via the Schrödinger equation. |
| 32 | Schrödinger Equation (Generalized) | Dynamics & Time Evolution | The equation of motion for quantum states: iħ d\|Ψ⟩/dt = H\|Ψ⟩. For expectation values of observables (Ehrenfest): d⟨L⟩/dt = –(i/ħ)⟨[L, H]⟩. The quantum analogue of Hamilton's equations. |
| 33 | Unitary Operator | Dynamics & Time Evolution | An operator U satisfying U†U = I (and UU† = I). Time evolution is unitary: \|Ψ(t)⟩ = e^{-iHt/ħ}\|Ψ(0)⟩. Unitary operators preserve inner products: ⟨Φ(t)\|Ψ(t)⟩ = ⟨Φ(0)\|Ψ(0)⟩. |
| 34 | Conservation Law | Dynamics & Time Evolution | An observable Q is conserved if it commutes with the Hamiltonian: [Q, H] = 0. Energy (H itself) is always conserved since [H, H] = 0. Conservation means expectation values of all functions of Q are time-independent. |
| 35 | Spin in a Magnetic Field | Spin Systems | The Hamiltonian for a spin in a magnetic field B is proportional to σ·B. Leads to Larmor precession: the spin expectation value vector precesses around the magnetic field direction at frequency ω (the Larmor frequency). |
| 36 | Uncertainty (Standard Deviation) ΔA | Uncertainty | The root-mean-square deviation of measurement outcomes: (ΔA)² = ⟨(A – ⟨A⟩)²⟩ = ⟨A²⟩ – ⟨A⟩². Quantifies the statistical spread of results. Zero only when the system is in an eigenstate of A. |
| 37 | Generalized Uncertainty Principle | Uncertainty | For any two observables A and B: ΔA ΔB ≥ (1/2)\|⟨[A, B]⟩\|. The product of uncertainties is bounded below by half the expectation of the commutator. Heisenberg's position-momentum uncertainty is a special case. |
| 38 | Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle | Uncertainty | A special case of the generalized principle: Δx Δp ≥ ħ/2. Position and momentum cannot be simultaneously known with arbitrary precision. Derived from the canonical commutator [x, p] = iħ. |
| 39 | Simultaneously Measurable Observables | Uncertainty | Two observables can be measured simultaneously without mutual disturbance if and only if they commute: [A, B] = 0. Commuting observables share a complete set of eigenvectors. Non-commuting observables (like σ_x and σ_z) cannot be known simultaneously. |
| 40 | Tensor Product Space | Composite Systems | The mathematical construction for combining independent quantum systems into a composite system. For two subsystems of dimensions N_A and N_B, the tensor product space has dimension N_A × N_B. Basis vectors: \|ab⟩ = \|a⟩ ⊗ \|b⟩. |
| 41 | Product State | Composite Systems | A state of a composite system where each subsystem is prepared independently: \|Ψ⟩ = \|Ψ_A⟩ ⊗ \|Ψ_B⟩. Each subsystem behaves exactly as it would in isolation. Product states have 4 real parameters for two spins (2 per spin). |
| 42 | Entanglement | Composite Systems | A quantum correlation with no classical analogue. An entangled state cannot be factorized as a product state \|Ψ_A⟩ ⊗ \|Ψ_B⟩. The general two-spin state has 6 real parameters — 2 more than product states. Knowing everything about the composite system does not imply knowing anything about its parts. |
| 43 | Maximally Entangled State | Composite Systems | A state with the strongest possible entanglement, where nothing is known about individual subsystems despite complete knowledge of the composite system. The singlet state and three triplet states are maximally entangled. |
| 44 | Singlet State \|sing⟩ | Composite Systems | The maximally entangled two-spin state: \|sing⟩ = (1/√2)(\|ud⟩ − \|du⟩). Verified at source line 7438–7440. For the singlet, measurements along any axis yield perfectly anti-correlated results: σ_xτ_x\|sing⟩ = σ_yτ_y\|sing⟩ = σ_zτ_z\|sing⟩ = –\|sing⟩ (lines 7860–7968). |
| 45 | Triplet States \|T₁⟩, \|T₂⟩, \|T₃⟩ | Composite Systems | Three maximally entangled symmetric states: \|T₁⟩ = (1/√2)(\|ud⟩ + \|du⟩), \|T₂⟩ = (1/√2)(\|uu⟩ + \|dd⟩), \|T₃⟩ = (1/√2)(\|uu⟩ − \|dd⟩). Together with the singlet, form the Bell basis for two qubits. |
| 46 | Composite Observables | Composite Systems | Observables formed from products of individual subsystem operators, e.g., σ_z ⊗ τ_z. Can only be measured by comparing results from both detectors. For the singlet state, σ_xτ_x\|sing⟩ = –\|sing⟩, σ_yτ_y\|sing⟩ = –\|sing⟩, σ_zτ_z\|sing⟩ = –\|sing⟩. |
| 47 | Alice and Bob Observables | Composite Systems | Operators that act on only one subsystem. σ acts on Alice's half (ignoring Bob's label); τ acts on Bob's half. All of Alice's operators commute with all of Bob's — measurements on separate subsystems never interfere. |
| 48 | Hidden Variables | Quantum Foundations | The hypothesis that quantum states are incomplete descriptions and additional (hidden) parameters exist that would restore determinism. Variant A: hidden variables are measurable in principle. Variant B: hidden variables are in principle undetectable. The singlet state's perfect correlations across all measurement axes challenge hidden-variable theories. |
| 49 | [NGOẠI SUY] Wave-Particle Duality | Quantum Foundations | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 8, page 235+). Quantum entities exhibit both particle-like and wave-like behavior. The wave function ψ(x) yields probability density \|ψ(x)\|² for particle position. Wave-like interference and quantized energy levels arise from boundary conditions on the wave function. |
| 50 | [PARTIAL] Wave Function ψ(x) | States & Vectors | Abstract definition verified at source lines 6071–6192 (Lecture 5): ψ(a,b,c,...) = ⟨a,b,c,...\|Ψ⟩ — the set of coefficients in an eigenfunction expansion, with P = ψ*ψ. Position-space representation ψ(x) = ⟨x\|Ψ⟩ with probability density \|ψ(x)\|² is from Lecture 8 (page 235+, not in source). |
| 51 | [NGOẠI SUY] Momentum Operator | Operators & Observables | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 8–9, page 235+). In position representation: p = –iħ ∂/∂x. The canonical commutator is [x, p] = iħ. Eigenstates of momentum are plane waves e^{ipx/ħ}. |
| 52 | [NGOẠI SUY] Hamiltonian for a Particle | Dynamics & Time Evolution | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 8–9, page 235+). For a single particle in a potential V(x): H = p²/(2m) + V(x) = –(ħ²/2m) ∂²/∂x² + V(x). Generates the Schrödinger equation: iħ ∂ψ/∂t = –(ħ²/2m) ∂²ψ/∂x² + V(x)ψ. |
| 53 | [NGOẠI SUY] Harmonic Oscillator | Dynamics & Time Evolution | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 10, page 311+). A system with Hamiltonian H = p²/(2m) + (mω²/2)x². Energy levels are quantized: E_n = ħω(n + 1/2) for n = 0, 1, 2, .... The ground state has zero-point energy ħω/2. Creation and annihilation operators (a†, a) raise/lower the energy level. |
| 54 | [NGOẠI SUY] Zero-Point Energy | Dynamics & Time Evolution | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 10, page 311+). The ground state energy E₀ = ħω/2 of the harmonic oscillator — non-zero even at absolute zero temperature. A direct consequence of the uncertainty principle: if position and momentum were both exactly zero, ΔxΔp ≥ ħ/2 would be violated. |
| 55 | [NGOẠI SUY] Creation Operator a† / Annihilation Operator a | Operators & Observables | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 10, page 311+). Ladder operators for the harmonic oscillator: a = (1/√2)(x + ip), a† = (1/√2)(x – ip). a† raises the energy level: a†\|n⟩ = √(n+1)\|n+1⟩. a lowers it: a\|n⟩ = √n\|n−1⟩. Satisfy [a, a†] = 1. |
| 56 | [NGOẠI SUY] Particle in a Box | Dynamics & Time Evolution | NOT IN SOURCE (Lecture 8–9, page 235+). A particle confined to a region 0 ≤ x ≤ L with infinite potential walls. Energy levels: E_n = n²π²ħ²/(2mL²). Wave functions: ψ_n(x) = √(2/L) sin(nπx/L). Demonstrates quantization from boundary conditions. |

The term "density matrix" or "reduced density matrix" does not appear in the source pages. |
| 58 | Einstein-Bohr Debate | Quantum Foundations | Einstein argued quantum mechanics was incomplete (EPR paradox, "God does not play dice"). Bohr defended the completeness of quantum descriptions. The debate centered on entanglement: Einstein showed that knowing everything about a composite system may imply knowing nothing about its parts. The issue remains open. |
| 59 | Determinism vs Probability | Quantum Foundations | Classical mechanics is deterministic; quantum mechanics is inherently probabilistic. The Born rule gives only probabilities, not certainties, for most measurement outcomes. Susskind adopts the pragmatic view: quantum mechanics is a complete calculus of probabilities. |
| 60 | Reversibility and Information Conservation | Dynamics & Time Evolution | The quantum analogue of the classical "minus-first law": information is never lost. Unitary evolution ensures that distinct initial states evolve to distinct states at all times, both future and past. This is encoded in U†U = I. |
| 61 | Classical Limit (ħ → 0) | Quantum Foundations | Classical mechanics emerges when ħ is negligibly small. In this limit, commutators approach zero: (i/ħ)[F, G] → {F, G} (Poisson bracket). Observables approximately commute, recovering deterministic, Boolean classical physics. |
| 62 | Poisson Bracket ↔ Commutator | Dynamics & Time Evolution | The bridge between classical and quantum mechanics: [F, G] ↔ iħ{F, G}. In the classical limit, the quantum commutator divided by iħ becomes the classical Poisson bracket. This correspondence underlies canonical quantization. |
| 63 | [PARTIAL] Canonical Quantization | Quantum Foundations | The commutator↔Poisson bracket correspondence [F, G] ↔ iħ{F, G} is verified at lines 5174–5182. The term "canonical quantization" itself does not appear in the source — it is standard QM terminology for the procedure of replacing classical observables with Hermitian operators and Poisson brackets with commutators. |
| 64 | Complex Numbers as Necessity | States & Vectors | Complex numbers in quantum mechanics are not a convenience but a necessity. Spin states along the y-axis (σ_y = ±1) require imaginary components. No representation of spin states using only real numbers can reproduce all experimental predictions. |
| 65 | Degrees of Freedom (Counting Parameters) | States & Vectors | A single spin state is specified by 2 real parameters (after normalization and ignoring global phase), matching the two angles needed to specify a direction in 3D space. A general two-spin state has 6 real parameters vs 4 for a product state — the extra 2 quantify entanglement. |
