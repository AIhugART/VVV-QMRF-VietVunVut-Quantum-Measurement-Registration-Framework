# Buddhist Epistemology — Complete Concept Reference

## 1. Valid Sources of Knowledge (Pramāṇa)

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pratyakṣa / Paccakkha | Direct Perception | Knowledge acquired directly through the senses or introspection, without the mediation of inference or language |
| 2 | Anumāna | Inference | Knowledge derived by reasoning from observable signs or evidence to an unobserved conclusion |
| 3 | Āgama / Śabda | Scriptural Testimony | Knowledge accepted on the authority of reliable speakers — the Buddha, enlightened teachers, or canonical texts |
| 4 | Upamāna | Analogical Reasoning | Knowledge obtained through comparison or analogy; accepted by some schools, rejected by others |

## 2. Objects of Cognition (Viṣaya / Ālambana)

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | Svalakṣaṇa | Particular / Unique Characteristic | The momentary, individual object as it actually exists; the proper object of direct perception |
| 6 | Sāmānyalakṣaṇa | Universal / General Characteristic | An abstract, constructed category shared across individuals; the object of inferential and conceptual cognition |
| 7 | Dharma | Dharma (Experiential Datum) | The minimal unit of experienced reality; what can be cognized in its irreducible form |
| 8 | Nimitta | Cognitive Sign / Mark | The feature or image that the mind grasps during a perceptual act; the mental aspect of an object |
| 9 | Ālambana | Cognitive Object | Any object — internal or external — that serves as the support or basis for an arising cognition |

## 3. Structure of the Knowing Mind (Vijñāna / Citta)

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | Vijñāna / Viññāṇa | Consciousness / Discriminating Awareness | Awareness that distinguishes objects through the six sense faculties (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind) |
| 11 | Citta | Mind / Mental Continuum | The stream of mental events that constitutes the knowing subject; no fixed "self" underlies it |
| 12 | Ālayavijñāna | Store-House Consciousness | In Yogācāra: the subliminal repository of cognitive seeds (bīja) that conditions future experience |
| 13 | Manovijñāna | Mental Consciousness | The faculty that synthesizes sense data, performs judgment, and generates conceptual thought |
| 14 | Manas | Defiled Intellect | The faculty that misapprehends the store-house consciousness as a permanent self, distorting cognition |
| 15 | Cetanā | Volition / Intention | The mental factor that directs attention and shapes how cognition unfolds |
| 16 | Manasikāra | Attention / Mental Engagement | The act of turning the mind toward an object; a precondition for any cognition to arise |

## 4. Cognitive Errors and Illusion (Bhrānti / Vipallāsa)

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 17 | Vipallāsa / Viparyāsa | Cognitive Inversion / Distortion | Systematic misperception of reality under four headings: permanence, pleasure, self, and purity |
| 18 | Anicca mistaken as Nicca | Permanence Illusion | Perceiving impermanent phenomena as permanent; the root temporal error in cognition |
| 19 | Dukkha mistaken as Sukha | Pleasure Illusion | Perceiving inherently unsatisfactory experience as genuine happiness |
| 20 | Anattā mistaken as Attā | Self Illusion | Projecting a fixed, substantial self onto a stream of impersonal processes |
| 21 | Asubha mistaken as Subha | Purity Illusion | Perceiving the body and conditioned phenomena as inherently pure or attractive |
| 22 | Avidyā / Avijjā | Ignorance / Nescience | Fundamental not-knowing of the three marks; the epistemic root of all cognitive distortion |
| 23 | Māyā | Illusion / Apparition | A phenomenal appearance that misleads cognition because it does not correspond to ultimate reality |
| 24 | Moha | Delusion | The mental factor of confusion and obscuration that prevents clear apprehension of objects |
| 25 | Micchā-diṭṭhi | Wrong View | A formed, propositional belief that misrepresents reality (e.g., eternalism, nihilism) |

## 5. The Cognitive Process (Citta-vīthi / Perceptual Sequence)

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | Citta-vīthi | Cognitive Series / Mind-Moment Stream | The sequence of discrete mental moments that process a single object from initial contact to apperception |
| 27 | Phassa / Sparśa | Contact | The convergence of sense faculty, object, and consciousness; the necessary trigger for any cognitive episode |
| 28 | Vedanā | Feeling Tone | The hedonic quality (pleasant, unpleasant, neutral) that colours every cognitive moment |
| 29 | Saññā / Samjñā | Perception / Recognition | The mental factor that identifies, categorizes, and labels objects based on prior experience |
| 30 | Vitarka | Initial Application of Thought | The first moment of directing discursive thought onto an object; a bridge between perception and reflection |
| 31 | Vicāra | Sustained Examination | Continued, exploratory attention to an object already apprehended; refines initial cognition |

## 6. Modes of Knowing (Jñāna / Ñāṇa)

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 32 | Nirvikalpa-jñāna | Non-Conceptual Cognition | Direct apprehension of a particular without the overlay of concepts, names, or categories |
| 33 | Savikalpa-jñāna | Conceptual Cognition | Cognition mediated by concepts, linguistic categories, and mental constructs |
| 34 | Prajñā / Paññā | Wisdom / Liberating Insight | The penetrative knowledge that sees the three marks directly and uproots cognitive distortion |
| 35 | Yoniso manasikāra | Wise Attention / Thorough Consideration | Attending to experience in a way that tracks its actual nature; epistemically productive attention |
| 36 | Abhijñā | Higher Direct Knowledge | Trans-ordinary cognitive capacities (e.g., recollection of past lives, telepathy) developed through meditation |
| 37 | Āsevita-jñāna | Cultivated Knowledge | Knowledge that must be repeatedly practiced and developed to become reliable, not merely intellectually grasped |

## 7. Foundational Metaphysical Commitments with Epistemic Consequences

| # | Concept (Sanskrit / Pali) | English Term | Epistemic Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 38 | Anicca | Impermanence | Objects exist only as momentary events; cognition can never capture a static entity |
| 39 | Anattā / Anātman | No-Self | There is no permanent knowing subject; the "cognizer" is itself a constructed series of processes |
| 40 | Śūnyatā | Emptiness | All phenomena, including cognitive acts and their objects, lack inherent self-existence; all knowledge is perspectival |
| 41 | Pratītyasamutpāda | Dependent Origination | Cognitive events arise in dependence on conditions, not from an autonomous mind or independent world |
| 42 | Saṃvṛti-satya | Conventional Truth | The epistemic level at which ordinary language, perception, and inference validly operate |
| 43 | Paramārtha-satya | Ultimate Truth | The epistemic level at which phenomena are seen without conceptual superimposition; accessible only to non-conceptual wisdom |

## 8. School-Specific Epistemic Doctrines

| # | School | Key Epistemic Doctrine | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 44 | Vaibhāṣika | Direct Realism | External objects exist independently and are perceived directly without an intervening mental image |
| 45 | Sautrāntika | Representationalism | External objects exist but are known only indirectly through mental representations (ākāra) |
| 46 | Yogācāra / Vijñānavāda | Idealism (Mind-Only) | No external objects exist; apparent objects are projections of consciousness; only mental events are real |
| 47 | Madhyamaka | Anti-Foundationalism | Both subject and object lack inherent existence; conventional cognition is valid within its frame but not ultimately grounded |
| 48 | Dignāga–Dharmakīrti (Pramāṇavāda) | Buddhist Epistemology Proper | Systematic theory holding only two valid sources (perception and inference); truth is defined by causal efficacy (arthakriyā) |
