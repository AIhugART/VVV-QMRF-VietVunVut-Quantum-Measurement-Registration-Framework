# Buddhist Epistemology — Key Concepts

| Concept | Pali / Sanskrit | Category | Definition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Valid cognition | Pramāṇa | Foundation | A reliable source of knowledge; the means by which correct cognition is attained |
| Direct perception | Pratyakṣa | Source of knowledge | Immediate cognition through the senses, prior to inference or conceptual overlay |
| Inference | Anumāna | Source of knowledge | Indirect knowledge obtained through logical reasoning based on a sign (liṅga) |
| Testimony | Āgama / Śabda | Source of knowledge | Knowledge derived from a reliable authority; in Buddhism, the word of the Buddha |
| Object of cognition | Prameya | Foundation | The reality that serves as the object of valid knowledge |
| Erroneous cognition | Bhrānti / Viparyaya | Epistemic error | A mistaken apprehension of reality; an inversion of the true nature of an object |
| Doubt | Saṃśaya | Epistemic error | A state of indecision in which neither alternative is definitively established |
| Conceptual construction | Vikalpa / Kalpanā | Mental structure | Conceptualized, categorizing cognition that applies names and classes — contrasted with bare perception |
| Non-conceptual perception | Nirvikalpaka pratyakṣa | Mental structure | Pure, direct perception free from conceptual or linguistic elaboration |
| Mental perception | Mānasa pratyakṣa | Type of perception | Inner cognition through mental consciousness, independent of the five external sense faculties |
| Self-awareness | Svasaṃvedana | Type of perception | The reflexive dimension of consciousness by which the mind is aware of its own states |
| Yogic perception | Yogipratyakṣa | Type of perception | Direct, supra-ordinary perception arising from deep meditative cultivation; seeing ultimate reality |
| Particular / Unique mark | Svalakṣaṇa | Ontology–cognition | The individual, unique characteristic of a thing; the object of direct perception |
| Universal / General mark | Sāmānyalakṣaṇa | Ontology–cognition | A shared, general characteristic; the object of inference and conceptual thought |
| Exclusion | Apoha | Philosophy of language | Meaning is conveyed by excluding what something is not (not-non-cow → cow); Dignāga's theory of linguistic reference |
| Sign / Logical reason | Liṅga / Hetu | Logic | The inferential mark or logical reason used to derive a conclusion (e.g., smoke → fire) |
| Thesis | Pakṣa | Logic | The proposition to be established in a Buddhist syllogism |
| Triple-condition syllogism | Trairūpya | Logic | The three conditions for a valid reason: presence in the thesis, presence in similar cases, absence in dissimilar cases |
| Ignorance | Avidyā / Avijjā | Epistemic obstacle | Fundamental non-knowing of the true nature of reality; the root of suffering and cyclic existence |
| Wisdom | Prajñā / Paññā | Liberation | Penetrating insight into the nature of reality; the path out of ignorance |
| Emptiness | Śūnyatā | Ultimate object | The absence of inherent existence in self and phenomena; ultimate reality according to Madhyamaka |
| Two truths | Dvisatya | Ontology–cognition | Conventional truth (saṃvṛti) and ultimate truth (paramārtha); two levels at which reality is cognized |
