# VVV-EQM: An Epistemic Closure Framework for Quantum Measurement

## Abstract

Quantum measurement remains conceptually significant because quantum states are represented probabilistically, while measurement events are registered as definite outcomes (Krips 2010; Schlosshauer 2004). Existing physical formalisms remain successful, yet the ontology of observation — the relation among object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity — remains open to further conceptual clarification. This paper proposes VVV-EQM as an epistemic closure framework for interpreting the structure of quantum measurement. Within this framework, measurement is interpreted as a structured process in which a measurement-relevant appearance becomes registered and stabilized as a valid definite outcome. This paper does not claim that Buddhist Epistemology provides a physical mechanism for wavefunction collapse. Rather, it proposes that Buddhist Epistemology offers bounded ontological and epistemological resources for reinterpreting the structure of quantum measurement.

## Keywords

Quantum measurement; Buddhist Epistemology; epistemic closure; pramāṇa; observer; measurement validity; philosophy of quantum mechanics; ontology of observation.

## 1. Introduction

Quantum measurement presents a central conceptual difficulty in the interpretation of quantum theory. Quantum states are represented through probabilistic structures, while measurement events are registered as definite outcomes (Krips 2010; Myrvold 2020). This contrast does not by itself imply a failure of quantum formalism. Rather, it indicates a continuing need to clarify how state, measurement interaction, registration, observer, and outcome are conceptually related.

The present paper focuses on this conceptual layer. It does not argue that quantum mechanics lacks predictive success, nor does it propose a new physical dynamics of collapse. Instead, it examines the ontology of observation: the conditions under which object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity converge into what is accepted as a definite measurement outcome.

To address this issue, the paper proposes VVV-EQM, an epistemic closure framework for quantum measurement. Drawing on Buddhist Epistemology, especially the analysis of valid cognition in the Dignāga-Dharmakīrti pramāṇa tradition, the framework interprets measurement as a structured process in which a measurement-relevant appearance becomes stabilized as a valid outcome (Tillemans 2026). The paper treats this relation as a bounded interpretive and ontological framework, not as an empirical replacement for quantum mechanics.

## 2. The Measurement Problem as a Structural Problem

The measurement problem is often introduced through the contrast between probabilistic state description and definite measurement outcome (Krips 2010; Schlosshauer 2004). Prior to measurement, the system is described in terms of possible outcomes. After measurement, a specific result is registered. The conceptual difficulty lies not merely in the existence of probability, but in the relation between probabilistic description, measurement interaction, registration, and outcome.

In this paper, measurement is treated as a structural problem. A measurement event involves a quantum state, a measurement condition, a system of registration, and a result that is accepted as definite. The term "observer" is therefore used cautiously. It does not automatically refer to human consciousness; in measurement discussions, it may involve apparatus, registration, or an observing system depending on interpretation (Krips 2010; Rovelli 1996; Faye 2023). In this paper, it refers to the condition under which a result becomes registered, accessible, and intelligible as an outcome.

This distinction is essential. If "observer" is left ambiguous, the analysis can easily shift from a conceptual account of measurement into an unsupported claim that consciousness physically causes collapse. VVV-EQM avoids this move by distinguishing physical interaction, registration, cognition, appearance, and validity.

## 3. Observation, Registration, and Validity

Observation is not a simple term. In the context of quantum measurement, it may refer to physical interaction, apparatus registration, epistemic access, or the interpretation of an outcome (Krips 2010; Faye 2023). These meanings are often compressed into a single word, producing conceptual ambiguity.

VVV-EQM begins by separating these layers. "Registration" refers to the condition by which a measurement-relevant event becomes recorded or stabilized as an accessible outcome. "Appearance" refers to the way such an event becomes presentable as a determinate result. "Cognition" refers to the epistemic structure through which an appearance becomes accessible as something known or interpreted. "Validity" refers to the condition by which the registered result counts as a legitimate measurement outcome.

The underlying issue is therefore not the mathematical success of quantum mechanics, but the under-specified ontology of observation. Without distinguishing registration, appearance, cognition, and validity, the relation between measurement and definite outcome remains conceptually compressed.

## 4. Buddhist Epistemology as a Conceptual Resource

Buddhist Epistemology, especially the Dignāga-Dharmakīrti pramāṇa tradition, provides a rigorous tradition for analyzing valid cognition, perception, inference, and the relation between cognition and object (Tillemans 2026; Dreyfus 1997; Arnold n.d.). In the present paper, these resources are not introduced as physical variables or as replacements for quantum theory. They are introduced as conceptual tools for clarifying the epistemic structure of measurement.

The concept of "pramāṇa" concerns reliable or valid cognition. In Dharmakīrti's epistemology, perception ("pratyakṣa") and inference ("anumāna") are central sources of knowledge, while "artha-kriyā" is linked to causal or practical efficacy (Tillemans 2026; Dunne 2004; Katsura 1984; Encyclopedia of Buddhism n.d.). In VVV-EQM, these concepts provide resources for examining why a registered result counts as valid rather than merely occurring, how an outcome becomes directly presentable, how it is interpreted, and how it becomes operationally meaningful.

These mappings are bounded. The paper does not claim that Buddhist Epistemology is equivalent to quantum physics. It claims only that Buddhist Epistemology may clarify the epistemic structure through which object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity become related.

## 5. The VVV-EQM Framework

VVV-EQM defines quantum measurement as "epistemic closure": a structured process in which object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity converge into a definite outcome under defined measurement conditions.

The framework contains six primary components. The "epistemic field" is the structured condition within which object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity can be related. "Object" refers to what is measured or appears as the target of measurement within this structure. "Appearance" refers to the way a measurement-relevant event becomes presentable as a determinate result. "Cognition" refers to the knowing structure through which an appearance becomes accessible as a result. "Registration" refers to the condition by which a measurement event becomes recorded or stabilized. "Validity" refers to the condition by which the registered outcome counts as legitimate.

These components are not proposed as new physical entities. The "epistemic field" is not a physical field. "Cognition" is not a physical cause of wavefunction collapse. "Validity" is not a hidden variable. The function of the framework is interpretive and ontological: it clarifies how a measurement outcome becomes intelligible as a valid result.

## 6. Measurement as Epistemic Closure

In VVV-EQM, epistemic closure occurs when a measurement-relevant appearance becomes registered and stabilized as a valid definite outcome. The process may be represented as follows:

```text
Measurement condition
→ Object / Appearance relation
→ Registration
→ Validity
→ Definite outcome
→ Epistemic closure
```

This sequence does not describe the physical dynamics of wavefunction collapse. It describes the epistemic structure through which a measurement outcome becomes intelligible. A physical interaction may occur, but it becomes a measurement outcome only when it is registered, accessible, and valid within a measurement context.

The concept of epistemic closure therefore addresses the root cause identified in this paper: the tendency to assume observer and object as already independent before analyzing the conditions under which observation becomes meaningful. VVV-EQM reverses this order. It first analyzes the epistemic conditions that allow object, appearance, registration, cognition, and validity to converge.

## 7. Structural Mapping

The mappings proposed here are not claims of identity between Buddhist Epistemology and quantum physics. They are structural and interpretive correspondences intended to clarify how measurement outcomes become intelligible as valid results.

| Buddhist Epistemology | Quantum Measurement | Relation Type | Boundary |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Pramāṇa" | Measurement validity | Interpretive mapping | Not physical equivalence |
| "Pratyakṣa" | Observation / registration | Analogy + mapping | Not identical to detector event |
| "Anumāna" | Interpretation of result | Structural analogy | Not replacement for statistical inference |
| "Artha-kriyā" | Operational efficacy | Structural resemblance | Not same doctrine |
| Object-cognition relation | Observer-system relation | Ontological comparison | Not empirical identity |
| Appearance | Definite outcome appearance | Interpretive mapping | Not illusion |
| Valid cognition | Valid measurement result | Framework mapping | Not physical production |
| "Epistemic closure" | Definite outcome stabilization | Framework proposal | Not collapse mechanism |

This table is central to the framework because it prevents category error. The relation between Buddhist Epistemology and quantum measurement is not equivalence. It is a bounded structural correspondence.

## 8. Discussion and Implications

VVV-EQM clarifies quantum measurement at the conceptual level by distinguishing physical interaction, registration, appearance, cognition, and validity. Its main contribution is not to explain the physical mechanism of collapse, but to show that a definite outcome can be analyzed as the result of epistemic closure.

This framework may be useful for philosophy of quantum mechanics because it provides a structured way to discuss the observer without reducing the observer to human consciousness. It also provides a way to discuss validity without introducing a new physical variable. By drawing on Buddhist Epistemology, VVV-EQM brings attention to the conditions under which an object becomes available to cognition and under which a result counts as valid.

The framework may be compared in future work with interpretations such as Copenhagen-style accounts, QBism, relational quantum mechanics, decoherence-based approaches, and other observer-sensitive interpretations (Faye 2023; Fuchs, Mermin, and Schack 2014; Rovelli 1996; Schlosshauer 2004). Such comparison is beyond the scope of the present paper.

## 9. Limitations and Future Work

This paper has several limitations. First, it does not provide a physical mechanism for wavefunction collapse. Second, it does not introduce a new mathematical formalism. Third, it does not offer experimental predictions. Fourth, it does not replace quantum mechanics or any established physical formalism. Fifth, the mapping between Buddhist Epistemology and quantum measurement remains interpretive and structural.

These limitations are necessary because the present paper is a conceptual framework paper. Its purpose is to clarify the epistemic structure of measurement, not to establish a new physical theory.

Future work may develop a formal model of epistemic closure, compare VVV-EQM with existing interpretations of quantum mechanics, and examine specific measurement scenarios. Further work should also provide more detailed source-grounded analysis of Buddhist Epistemology, especially "pramāṇa", "pratyakṣa", "anumāna", and "artha-kriyā".

## 10. Conclusion

This paper has proposed VVV-EQM as an epistemic closure framework for quantum measurement. The framework begins from the observation that quantum measurement involves a transition from probabilistic description to definite outcome. It identifies the root cause of conceptual ambiguity as an under-specified ontology of observation, especially the relation among object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity.

VVV-EQM interprets measurement as a process in which a measurement-relevant appearance becomes registered and stabilized as a valid definite outcome. This interpretation is grounded in Buddhist Epistemology as a conceptual resource, not as a physical theory.

The paper does not claim that Buddhist Epistemology provides a physical mechanism for wavefunction collapse. Its contribution is more limited and more precise: it offers a bounded ontological and epistemological framework for reinterpreting the structure of quantum measurement.
