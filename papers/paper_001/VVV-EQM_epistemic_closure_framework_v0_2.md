## Abstract

Quantum measurement remains conceptually significant because quantum states are represented through probabilistic structures, while measurement events are registered as definite outcomes. Existing quantum formalisms remain empirically successful, yet the ontology of observation — the relation among object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity — remains conceptually compressed when the term "observer" is left undefined. This paper proposes VVV-EQM as an epistemic closure framework for interpreting the structure of quantum measurement. The framework does not treat observation as a mysterious mental force or as a replacement for physical interaction. Instead, it distinguishes the physical occurrence of a measurement interaction from the epistemic structure through which an event becomes registered, accessible, and valid as a definite outcome. Drawing on Buddhist Epistemology, especially the Dignāga-Dharmakīrti analysis of valid cognition, perception, inference, object of cognition, conceptual construction, and causal efficacy, the paper argues that quantum measurement may be clarified as a bounded process of epistemic closure. This paper does not claim that Buddhist Epistemology provides a physical mechanism for wavefunction collapse. It proposes that Buddhist Epistemology offers an ontological and epistemological framework for reinterpreting the structure of quantum measurement.

## Keywords

Quantum measurement; Buddhist Epistemology; epistemic closure; pramāṇa; prameya; observer; measurement validity; ontology of observation; philosophy of quantum mechanics.

The correct claim level is therefore not "empirical solution" and not "physical model." The correct claim level is "interpretive mapping" with a bounded "ontological framework."

## 1. Introduction: The Measurement Tension

Quantum measurement presents a persistent conceptual tension in the interpretation of quantum theory. Before measurement, a quantum system is described by a state structure that assigns probabilities to possible outcomes. During measurement, a physical interaction occurs under specific conditions. After measurement, a definite outcome is registered.

This sequence is operationally familiar, but its interpretation remains difficult. The problem is not that quantum mechanics fails to calculate probabilities. The problem is that the transition from probabilistic description to definite registered outcome raises questions about what counts as observation, what counts as registration, what counts as an object of measurement, and what makes an outcome valid.

The word "observer" is therefore too compressed to carry the full conceptual burden. In ordinary language, an observer may be a person. In quantum measurement, however, the term may refer to apparatus, registration, interaction, information update, physical coupling, or an interpretation-dependent relation. If these meanings are not separated, discussion can mistakenly slide from a physical measurement problem into the unsupported claim that consciousness causes collapse.

This paper avoids that slide. It proposes VVV-EQM as an epistemic closure framework. The framework interprets measurement as a structured process in which a measurement-relevant appearance becomes registered and stabilized as a valid definite outcome. This is an interpretive and ontological framework, not a physical theory of collapse.

## 2. Research Problem and Scope

The central research problem is the under-specified ontology of observation in quantum measurement. A measurement event is not only a physical interaction. It is also an event that becomes intelligible as a result. The result must be recorded or stabilized, distinguished from non-results, interpreted within a measurement context, and accepted as valid.

The present paper asks one bounded question:

> Can Buddhist Epistemology provide a structural epistemic framework for clarifying how a measurement-relevant event becomes a valid definite outcome?

This question is deliberately limited. It does not ask whether Buddhist Epistemology can replace quantum mechanics. It does not ask whether Buddhist concepts can be inserted into quantum equations. It asks whether Buddhist Epistemology can clarify the structure of measurement as an epistemic event.

The scope of the paper is therefore conceptual. It examines how the Buddhist analysis of valid cognition can provide a framework for distinguishing object, appearance, cognition, registration, validity, and outcome. The paper uses Quantum Measurement as the mapped domain and Buddhist Epistemology as the primary ontological-epistemological frame.

## 3. Conceptual Background: Quantum Measurement

Quantum measurement is commonly introduced through the contrast between a probabilistic state description and a definite observed result. The formalism assigns probabilities to possible outcomes, but actual measurement produces a registered result. This creates interpretive pressure around the relation between state, measurement apparatus, observer, and outcome.

Different interpretations handle this pressure in different ways. Copenhagen-style accounts emphasize the role of measurement context and classical description. Decoherence-based approaches explain the suppression of interference through interaction with the environment. Relational and information-centered interpretations treat state and outcome as dependent on relations, agents, or information updates. These approaches are not rejected here. They are treated as successful or serious attempts to clarify different layers of the problem.

VVV-EQM addresses a narrower conceptual layer. It asks what structure allows an event to count as a valid outcome. This requires distinguishing at least five layers:

1. physical interaction;
2. appearance of a determinate result;
3. registration of that result;
4. interpretation of the result within a measurement context;
5. validation of the result as a legitimate outcome.

When these layers are compressed, "measurement" appears as a single event. When they are separated, measurement can be examined as a structured transition into epistemic closure.

In the project mapping, "Valid cognition" is represented by N_BE_00001 and includes the structure of means of knowing, valid cognition, object to be known, and resultant cognition. ## 5. Three-Why RCA of the Core Claim

### Claim

VVV-EQM proposes that quantum measurement can be interpreted as a process of epistemic closure.

### Symptom

The phrase "measurement outcome" is treated as obvious even though it combines physical interaction, registration, appearance, interpretation, and validity.

### Why 1: What directly makes this appear?

The term "observer" often combines several different meanings: physical apparatus, human observer, registration system, information access, and interpretive standpoint.

### Why 2: What deeper mechanism produces that?

The measurement discussion often focuses on the transition from state to outcome before fully distinguishing the epistemic layers through which an event becomes accepted as an outcome.

### Why 3: What hidden assumption makes the mechanism possible?

The hidden assumption is that observer and object can be treated as already independent and already defined before analyzing the conditions of observation, appearance, cognition, and validity.

### Root cause

The ontology of observation is under-specified: the relation among object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity is not sufficiently separated before the measurement outcome is interpreted.

### Fix

Introduce "epistemic closure" as a bounded conceptual framework that separates physical interaction from the epistemic conditions under which an event becomes a valid definite outcome.

### Boundary

The framework does not explain the physical dynamics of collapse. It clarifies the structure by which a measurement-relevant event becomes intelligible as a valid outcome.

### Verification

The framework is consistent with the project mapping in which N_BE_00001 relates valid cognition to object of cognition and resultant cognition, while direct perception, inference, conceptual construction, particularity, and causal efficacy are structurally distinguished.

## 6. Defining Epistemic Closure

In VVV-EQM, "epistemic closure" means the structured completion of a measurement-relevant process in which an event becomes registered, accessible, and valid as a definite outcome.

It does not mean logical closure in formal logic. It does not mean psychological certainty. It does not mean physical collapse. It means that the relevant epistemic conditions have converged so that a result can count as a legitimate outcome within a measurement context.

The framework can be stated in plain language:

> A measurement becomes epistemically closed when a measurement-relevant appearance is registered and validated as a definite outcome within a defined context.

This can be represented as a conceptual sequence:

```text
Measurement context
→ Measurement-relevant interaction
→ Appearance of a determinate result
→ Registration or stabilization
→ Validity condition
→ Definite outcome
→ Epistemic closure
```

This sequence is not a new physical law. It is a conceptual grammar for describing the epistemic side of measurement.

## 7. The VVV-EQM Framework

VVV-EQM contains four structural layers.

### 7.1 Potentiality Layer

The potentiality layer refers to the pre-outcome structure in which multiple possible results are relevant within a measurement context. In quantum mechanics, this is connected to probabilistic state description. In VVV-EQM, this layer is not identified with any Buddhist doctrine. It is simply the starting point of the mapped measurement problem.

The purpose of this layer is to avoid prematurely treating the outcome as already fixed. Before epistemic closure, the framework asks what conditions must be satisfied for one result to become validly registered.

### 7.2 Epistemic Interface

The epistemic interface is the structured relation among what is measured, the measurement condition, and the possibility of registration. It is where object and observation become related under a defined condition.

In Buddhist Epistemology, N_BE_00005, the object of cognition, is not isolated from the structure of valid cognition. The object must be related to a means of knowing. VVV-EQM uses this relation as a conceptual guide: a quantum measurement outcome should not be discussed only as an isolated object, but as an object-within-a-measurement-condition.

### 7.3 Appearance-Registration Layer

The appearance-registration layer distinguishes what appears as a determinate result from the act or condition by which that result is stabilized as accessible. This distinction matters because a physical interaction alone is not yet a valid outcome unless it becomes registered within the measurement arrangement.

Buddhist Epistemology distinguishes direct presentation from conceptual construction. N_BE_00002, direct perception, is contrasted with N_BE_00008, conceptual construction. VVV-EQM does not equate detector registration with Buddhist direct perception. It uses the distinction to separate presentation, registration, and later interpretation.

### 7.4 Validity / Closure Layer

The validity layer asks why a registered result counts as legitimate. In Buddhist Epistemology, N_BE_00001, valid cognition, is structurally linked to an object of cognition and to resultant cognition. VVV-EQM uses this structure to analyze why a measurement result is not merely an occurrence but a valid outcome.

Epistemic closure occurs when the registered appearance is accepted as valid within the measurement context. This is the framework's central claim.

## 9. Conceptual Notation

The framework may be written in a minimal conceptual notation, but this notation is not a physical equation.

Let:

```text
C = measurement context
O = object or target of measurement within C
A = measurement-relevant appearance
R = registration or stabilization condition
V = validity condition
D = definite outcome
EC = epistemic closure
```

Then VVV-EQM can be expressed as:

```text
EC occurs when, within C, O gives rise to A, A is stabilized by R,
and R satisfies V such that D counts as a valid outcome.
```

Or in compressed form:

```text
C(O → A → R → V) ⇒ D-as-valid-outcome
```

This notation has one purpose: to keep the conceptual structure explicit. It does not describe the physical transition from superposition to outcome. It does not modify the Schrödinger equation. It does not supply collapse dynamics. It only states the epistemic relation that VVV-EQM investigates.

## 10. What the Framework Clarifies

VVV-EQM clarifies three conceptual issues.

First, it separates "observer" from human consciousness. The observer-function in this framework is the condition under which an event becomes registered and valid as an outcome. It may involve apparatus, environment, record, interpretation, or epistemic access depending on the measurement context. It is not automatically a person or a mind.

Second, it separates physical interaction from epistemic closure. A physical interaction may occur without yet being analyzed as a valid outcome. Epistemic closure names the additional structure through which the event becomes registered, accessible, and valid.

Third, it separates appearance from validity. A result can appear or be recorded, but the framework still asks why that record counts as a valid outcome. This is where Buddhist Epistemology is useful: it already treats cognition, object, means of knowledge, and resultant cognition as structurally related rather than isolated.

## 11. Relation to Existing Interpretations

VVV-EQM should not be read as a rejection of existing interpretations. Its role is complementary and conceptual.

Compared with Copenhagen-style accounts, VVV-EQM shares concern for measurement context, but it does not depend on a strict classical-quantum cut. Compared with decoherence-based accounts, VVV-EQM accepts that environmental interaction may explain important physical features of outcome stability, but it does not treat decoherence alone as a full account of epistemic validity. Compared with relational quantum mechanics, VVV-EQM shares the idea that outcomes are relation-dependent, but it adds a specific epistemic structure of appearance, registration, and validity. Compared with QBism, VVV-EQM shares attention to the epistemic dimension, but it does not reduce the framework to personal belief or agent-centered probability.

These comparisons are preliminary. The present paper does not attempt a full interpretation-by-interpretation critique. It only positions VVV-EQM as a conceptual framework for analyzing the epistemic structure of measurement.

## 12. Limitations

This paper has strict limitations.

First, it does not provide a physical mechanism for wavefunction collapse.

Second, it does not introduce a new mathematical formalism for quantum dynamics.

Third, it does not offer experimental predictions.

Fourth, it does not prove that Buddhist Epistemology and quantum mechanics are equivalent.

Fifth, it does not claim that consciousness creates physical reality.

Sixth, it does not replace existing interpretations of quantum mechanics.

These limitations are not weaknesses of the paper. They define the correct claim level. Paper 1 is a conceptual and framework paper. Its task is to establish a disciplined structure that later papers may formalize, compare, or test.

## 13. Future Work

Future work should proceed in three stages.

First, a source-grounded Buddhist Epistemology paper should examine the concepts most important for VVV-EQM: pramāṇa, prameya, pratyakṣa, anumāna, vikalpa, svalakṣaṇa, sāmānyalakṣaṇa, and arthakriyā. This would strengthen the philosophical foundation.

Second, a comparative interpretation paper should test VVV-EQM against Copenhagen-style accounts, decoherence, relational quantum mechanics, QBism, and other observer-sensitive interpretations. This would clarify what VVV-EQM adds and what it merely restates.

Third, a formalization paper should ask whether epistemic closure can be expressed in a more rigorous model while preserving standard quantum predictions. Such a paper would need to show how the framework relates to existing formalism without inventing unsupported physics.

The next-order question is therefore:

> Can epistemic closure be formalized in a way that preserves standard quantum predictions while clarifying the epistemic structure of definite outcome validity?

## 14. Conclusion

This paper has proposed VVV-EQM as an epistemic closure framework for quantum measurement. It begins from the observation that quantum measurement involves a transition from probabilistic state description to definite registered outcome. It identifies the root cause of conceptual ambiguity as an under-specified ontology of observation, especially the compressed relation among object, appearance, cognition, registration, and validity.

The framework draws on Buddhist Epistemology not as a physical theory but as a disciplined conceptual resource. Concepts such as valid cognition, object of cognition, direct perception, inference, conceptual construction, particularity, and causal efficacy provide a way to analyze how a result becomes valid rather than merely occurring.

VVV-EQM therefore claims something limited but precise. It does not claim that Buddhist Epistemology solves wavefunction collapse. It claims that Buddhist Epistemology can help reinterpret the epistemic structure of quantum measurement by clarifying how a measurement-relevant appearance becomes registered and stabilized as a valid definite outcome.
